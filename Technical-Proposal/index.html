4. Understanding of the Assignment

IPPF has built, over successive phases, a Federation-wide analytics environment that already functions as a credible single source of truth: a Snowflake enterprise data warehouse fed through Azure Data Factory, drawing on eight institutional systems from DHIS2 service-delivery data to NetSuite financials, and surfaced through Power BI. That environment was designed, sensibly, for the Secretariat. The task now is different in kind, not merely in degree: to open that same data to roughly 120 Member Associations and a wider network of Collaborative Partners, across some 150 countries and four working languages, in a form they will actually use. We understand this assignment as the bridge between an analytically mature Secretariat platform and the operational reality of a diverse, decentralised Federation.

That framing carries three implications that shape everything we propose.

01

First, this is an extension, not a rebuild. The existing architecture is sound and represents significant prior investment. Our role is to build the Affiliate-facing layer on top of it new views, new access structures, new language support - while strengthening integration and data quality where that serves the Affiliate use cases. We will propose architectural change only where there is a clear, justified advantage, and we will say so plainly when there is not. The Secretariat dashboards continue to operate alongside what we build.

02

Second, the hard problems are about access and trust, not visualisation. Secretariat users authenticate through IPPF organisational accounts and single sign-on; Affiliate users sit outside that domain entirely, which makes external identity and access management a central design question rather than a deployment afterthought. Equally, a Federation-wide dataset raises real governance questions the moment Affiliates can see it: what each Affiliate is entitled to view, whether and how performance is compared across Affiliates, and how sensitive data is handled responsibly. IPPF has rightly left these to be defined with stakeholders. We treat governance, access, and benchmarking as a first-class design workstream, not a technical detail.

03

Third, adoption is the real measure of success. A dashboard that is launched but not used delivers nothing. Across 150 countries the Federation contains enormous variation in connectivity, data literacy, language, and analytical capacity. A single uniform rollout will reach the well-resourced Affiliates and lose the rest. The assignment therefore demands a training and adoption model designed for that variation from the outset which is why we have built our approach around tiered onboarding and a Training of Trainers model rather than a one-size training package.

We also recognise the constraints honestly. Much remains to be discovered: the precise user base, the priority use cases, the true state of data quality across the more bespoke source systems, and the governance rules the Federation will accept all emerge from the needs analysis, not before it. Our methodology is built to reduce that uncertainty deliberately and early, and to validate every major design decision with the people who will live with it.

5. Approach & Methodology

Our approach runs across the three Parts defined in the Terms of Reference, with two threads woven through all of them: continuous stakeholder validation, so that no major decision is taken without the people it affects; and iterative refinement, so that designs, builds, and training materials improve in response to real feedback rather than being delivered once and abandoned. We work primarily remotely, as the assignment anticipates, using structured asynchronous methods to reach Affiliates across time zones and languages.

1

Part 1 - Needs Analysis and Functional Specification

We begin with an inception phase to align with the Secretariat teams (PLI, MADDT, IT) on objectives, confirm the workplan, agree the consultation approach, and conduct an initial review of the existing environment and documentation. This produces the Inception Report and Workplan.

The needs analysis itself uses a tiered consultation model designed for a Federation too large and varied to engage uniformly. We propose a structured digital survey issued to all Affiliates in the four working languages, capturing current data use, decision-making needs, reporting priorities, technical capacity, and connectivity. The survey serves two purposes: it gathers Federation-wide signal, and it lets us stratify Affiliates by data maturity. From that stratification we select a representative sample across maturity levels, regions, and languages for deeper engagement - structured interviews and small-group sessions so that the design is informed by the full range of the Federation rather than only its loudest or best-resourced members. Indicatively, this comprises a survey to all ~120 Member Associations, approximately 30-45 deep-dive interviews across the maturity tiers, and consultations with around 10-15 Secretariat stakeholders to map data ownership, definitions, and constraints. The final consultation approach is confirmed at inception.

From these inputs we define and prioritise use cases, identify the indicators and data domains that matter to Affiliates (service delivery, financial performance, grants and others), and develop user journeys, dashboard structure, and illustrative design concepts. In parallel we open the governance and access workstream: proposing, for stakeholder decision, the access model (what each Affiliate sees), the approach to benchmarking and comparative reporting, and the handling of sensitive data. We validate findings and design concepts through structured engagement with selected stakeholders, refine on that feedback, and consolidate everything into a coherent design. This produces the Needs Analysis and Design Report.

Finally we consolidate all validated outputs into the Functional Specification Document: priority use cases, data requirements and indicators, dashboard structure and functionality, user-experience principles, and the governance and access framework - written to be implementation-ready so that Part 2 proceeds without rediscovery.

2

Part 2 - Development, Implementation and Iteration

Part 2 opens with implementation planning: we validate the technical approach against the existing architecture, confirm dependencies, risks, and integration requirements, and finalise the implementation plan. We expect to begin this in the closing stage of Part 1 where possible, so that technical discovery and specification overlap rather than running strictly end to end compressing the overall timeline without compromising validation.

We then strengthen the data environment. We review and validate the existing Snowflake and Azure Data Factory architecture, strengthen and expand integration across the source systems where the Affiliate use cases require it, validate indicator definitions, and address data-quality issues. We treat data quality as a defined activity rather than an assumption: an early data-quality assessment across the in-scope systems, with particular attention to the more bespoke and partially manual sources, so that issues are surfaced and addressed before they reach Affiliate users. The depth of integration work depends on the state of the systems, which is fully visible only after award; this is reflected in our risk and contingency approach.

Dashboard development then proceeds within the existing Power BI environment: building the Affiliate-facing views, visualisations, and navigation defined in the specification, implementing role-based access and row-level security so that each Affiliate sees what it is entitled to see, and building in multilingual support across the four working languages, including the right-to-left considerations Arabic requires. Critically, the access model for external Affiliate users who sit outside IPPF's domain - is designed and implemented here; we anticipate an approach based on Microsoft Entra External ID with a deployment and licensing model appropriate to Federation scale, confirmed against the user base established in Part 1.

We test iteratively. Selected stakeholders across maturity levels participate in structured user testing, and we refine the dashboard in cycles against their feedback on usability, performance, and how clearly the data reads rather than presenting a finished product for one-off sign-off. We then finalise and deploy within the existing environment, and prepare the technical documentation and user guidance that make the solution maintainable. This produces, in turn, the Implementation Plan, the Integrated and Validated Data Environment, the Tested and Refined Dashboard, and the Operational Dashboard and Documentation.

3

Part 3 - Rollout, Training and Adoption

Part 3 is where the investment is realised or lost, and we treat it with corresponding weight. Our approach is set out in full in Section 6.

Cross-cutting: governance, risk, and ways of working

Throughout, we work in close collaboration with the Secretariat teams, participate in regular check-ins, present interim findings and prototypes for feedback at each key stage, and incorporate designated reviewers' input before finalising deliverables. We maintain a live risk register from inception. The principal risks we anticipate, with our mitigations, are: variable data quality in the more bespoke source systems (mitigated by the early data-quality assessment); uneven Affiliate engagement in consultation (mitigated by asynchronous, multilingual methods and IPPF-coordinated access); the cost and complexity of Affiliate licensing and external authentication (mitigated by proposing a scalable, clearly costed model early and flagging the recurring cost for IPPF's budgeting); and the practical limits of right-to-left support in Power BI (mitigated by scoping the Arabic approach explicitly rather than assuming parity).

6. Training, Rollout and Adoption

The Terms of Reference rightly weight training and adoption heavily, and our experience bears out why: in a Federation this large and varied, the difference between a dashboard that is launched and one that is used comes down almost entirely to how adoption is designed. A uniform training package the same sessions, the same materials, delivered the same way to every Affiliate predictably reaches the well-resourced, well-connected, English-working Affiliates and loses everyone else. We have therefore built our approach around the diversity of the Federation rather than around an average Affiliate that does not exist.

A maturity-tiered Training of Trainers model

The needs-analysis survey in Part 1 stratifies Affiliates by data maturity, connectivity, and capacity. That same stratification drives the rollout, through three tiers.

Tier 1

The first tier comprises the higher-maturity Affiliates - those with existing data capacity and reliable connectivity. These receive advanced, direct training early, during the pilot stage of rollout, and become regional peer anchors: their trained focal points support neighbouring Affiliates in shared languages and contexts. This is the heart of the Training of Trainers model building Affiliate-led capacity that does not depend on us in perpetuity.

Tier 2

The second tier comprises the broad middle of the Federation. These Affiliates receive structured onboarding delivered partly through the trained Tier 1 peers and partly through self-paced modules hosted on the IPPF Academy in all four languages, reinforced by scheduled regional virtual sessions. This blend is deliberate: it scales across many Affiliates and time zones without depending on us delivering every session live.

Tier 3

The third tier comprises lower-capacity and lower-connectivity Affiliates, who are the most likely to be left behind by a conventional rollout and therefore receive the most deliberate support: simplified, role-focused materials, resources that work in low-bandwidth and offline-friendly formats, and direct support during the initial adoption period.

Materials, roles, and the IPPF Academy

For each defined user role we develop targeted materials rather than a single generic manual: concise role-based user guides, short task-focused video walkthroughs, and quick-reference cards for the handful of actions each role performs most. All materials are produced in English, French, Spanish, and Arabic, and hosted where appropriate on the IPPF Academy (Moodle) and the MA Forum, so that they live within the Federation's existing knowledge infrastructure and remain available to new staff long after rollout. This approach draws on direct experience: our Lead Consultant has trained ministry and stakeholder users on Power BI dashboards on prior public-sector and health-sector engagements, and our Power BI developer has led end-user training and deployment, so our rollout design is grounded in what works in practice. As part of the Training of Trainers model we define the roles and competencies expected of Affiliate focal points, equip nominated representatives to lead ongoing training and peer support within their own contexts, and deliver the ToT sessions for those representatives.

Support, feedback, and embedding

During the initial adoption period - indicatively the first eight weeks following launch, with fortnightly check-ins we provide structured support, troubleshooting and responding to user queries, and we build a feedback mechanism into the rollout itself, including within the dashboard, so that we hear from users who would never raise a formal ticket. We synthesise that feedback, recommend adjustments to improve usability and adoption, and refine both the materials and the onboarding approach in response to early experience. The objective throughout is not a launch event but embedding: a dashboard that becomes part of how Affiliates actually work, supported by a layer of trained peers across the Federation who can carry adoption forward after our engagement ends.

We note, finally and without overstating it, that a platform of this scale and diversity benefits from continuity of support beyond initial rollout as requirements evolve and Affiliate staff turn over. While that lies outside the scope of this assignment, we would be glad to discuss appropriate longer-term support arrangements with IPPF at the right stage.
